## 1. Learner-Centered Training Philosophy

The learner-centered approach contrasts with trainer-centered approaches. Instead of focusing on what the trainer wants to teach, it focuses on what learners need to learn.

### Four Core Principles

1. **Focus on content learners NEED**: Identify what problems learners need to solve, what's useful for them, and provide only essential content.

2. **Deliver content matching learner styles**: Learners have different learning styles (Visual, Auditory, Kinesthetic). Use diverse delivery methods rather than just one approach.

3. **Create opportunities for participation and discovery**: Learners retain more when they actively participate rather than passively listen. (Edgar Dale's retention research: 10% read, 20% hear, 30% see, 50% hear+see, 70% say, 90% say+do)

4. **Highlight benefits (WIIFM — What's In It For Me)**: Learners who don't see the benefit will be disengaged. State course benefits early to motivate participation.

### Interactive Learning
Maximize multi-directional interaction: Trainer↔Learner, Learner↔Learner. The more interaction dimensions, the better the learning outcomes.

## 2. ADDIE Model Overview

Five phases: **Analysis → Design → Development → Implementation → Evaluation & Revision**

Each phase produces specific outputs that feed the next:
- Analysis → Learner profile
- Design → Objectives + Content outline + Lesson plan
- Development → Slides + Learning activities + Materials
- Implementation → Pilot delivery
- Evaluation → Improvement recommendations

## 3. Step 1: Analysis — Learner Profiling

### Why Analyze Learners?
Understanding learners helps you: select appropriate objectives and content, diversify activities for different learning styles, leverage learner experience in the course.

### Learner Groups
- **Primary Learners**: The main target group the course is designed for
- **Secondary Learners**: Others who may also benefit; don't compromise the primary group's needs for them

### What to Analyze

**Training Needs** (why they need training):
- Fill knowledge/skill gaps for current job performance
- Solve specific workplace problems
- Acquire new skills for new responsibilities

**Learning Styles**:
- **Visual**: Learn by seeing — prefer reading, images, videos, diagrams
- **Auditory**: Learn by hearing — prefer lectures, discussions, shared experiences
- **Kinesthetic**: Learn by doing — prefer games, group work, case studies, hands-on practice

**Learning Conditions**:
- How much time can they dedicate?
- What environment will they learn in? (classroom, online, on-the-job)

### Learner Portrait (KSA Profile)
The output of analysis is a learner portrait describing three dimensions:
- **Attitude (A)**: Perspectives, views, and feelings about the training topic
- **Skills (S)**: Current abilities, techniques, and competencies
- **Knowledge (K)**: Existing understanding of the subject matter

### Using the Learner Portrait
The portrait informs ALL subsequent design decisions: what to include/exclude, how complex to make it, which methods to use, how much practice to include.

## 4. Step 2: Design — Objectives, Content, Lesson Plan

### 4.1 Writing Learning Objectives

Objectives must be learner-centered (what LEARNERS will be able to do, not what the trainer will cover).

**Benefits of clear objectives**: Learners know expectations, trainer stays focused, content selection has criteria, evaluators can measure results.

**Three Cognitive Levels** (from Bloom's taxonomy, simplified):

| Level | Description | Suitable For |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| 1. Understand & Retain | Grasp theory/concepts without needing to apply | Support roles |
| 2. Apply | Perform tasks or complete specific jobs | Execution roles |
| 3. Problem-Solve | Analyze, evaluate, propose improvements | Management/supervisory roles |

**Objective Formula**: Action Verb + Standard/Criteria + Condition

Components:
1. **Action Verb**: Must be observable and measurable. AVOID: "understand", "know", "appreciate", "be aware of". USE: "list", "demonstrate", "create", "analyze", "evaluate", "design"
2. **Condition**: Context in which the learner performs (e.g., "without supervisor support", "using the company template", "within 3 minutes")
3. **Standard/Criteria**: How well they must perform (e.g., "99% accuracy", "following company guidelines", "to customer satisfaction")

**Action Verb Examples by Level**:

Level 1 (Remember/Understand): Arrange, Cite, Estimate, Distinguish, Extract, Copy, Define, Describe, Repeat, Rewrite, Report, Illustrate, Complete, Execute, Classify, Explain, Summarize, State, Give examples, Identify, Point out, Label, List, Locate, Match, Adapt, Simulate, Discuss, Compare, Contrast, Organize

Level 2 (Apply): Prepare, Initiate, Adjust, Eliminate, Implement, Choose, Present, Use, Measure, Rotate, Repair, Assemble, Change, Select, Protect, Apply, Evaluate, Categorize, Modify, Draft, Recruit, Divide, Separate, Complete, Order

Level 3 (Analyze/Evaluate/Create): Distinguish, Specify, Prepare, Initiate, Adjust, Produce, Choose, Select, Eliminate, Implement, Present, Use, Measure, Evaluate, Rotate, Repair, Categorize, Modify, Organize, Plan

### 4.2 Selecting and Categorizing Content

**The Content Overload Problem**: Too much content, too little time. Solution: categorize by priority.

**Three Content Categories** (concentric circles model):
- **Must Have** (core): Without this, learners CANNOT achieve objectives. Always include.
- **Should Have** (middle ring): Supports easier achievement of objectives. Include if time permits.
- **Nice to Have** (outer ring): Useful but doesn't significantly impact objective achievement. Include only if ample time.

### 4.3 Content Sequencing

Content should follow a **logical sequence** for easy understanding and retention.

**The WHY-WHAT-HOW-SO WHAT Framework**:
1. **WHY** (Top-right): Why is this content important? What problem does it solve?
2. **WHAT** (Right): Present the content — explain so learners understand
3. **HOW** (Bottom): Practice — guide and create opportunities to apply
4. **SO WHAT** (Top-left): Real-world application reminders and caveats

**Delivery Techniques**:
- **Inductive (bottom-up)**: Start with specific examples/experiences → derive general principles. Good for experienced learners.
- **Deductive (top-down)**: Start with general principles/frameworks → apply to specific cases. Good for new content.

### 4.4 Building the Lesson Plan

The lesson plan is the facilitator's roadmap. It must specify:
- Session flow from start to finish
- Key content points and HOW to deliver each (inductive/deductive)
- Important emphasis points during explanation
- Discussion questions to encourage participation
- Activities for practicing/applying content
- Estimated time for each section
- Supporting resources (slides, handouts, references)

**Balance**: Not too brief (leaves facilitators lost) nor too detailed (kills creativity).

## 5. Step 3: Development — Slides and Activities

### 5.1 Slide Design Principles

**Principle 1 — Easy to Read (KISS)**: Keep It Short and Simple. Minimal text.

**Principle 2 — Easy to Understand**: Use meaningful images to replace text. Visuals aid comprehension and memory.

**Principle 3 — Easy to Remember**: Highlight key content using large fonts, bold/contrasting colors.

**Principle 4 — Dynamic/Engaging**:
- Combine warm and cool colors for contrast
- Warm colors ≈ 30% of slide (for focal points)
- Consistent color usage (same color for same content type)
- Use warm colors to draw attention to key elements

**Slide Production Checklist**:
- Create Slide Master for consistency
- Select illustrative images for content
- Link media (video, audio) where helpful
- Use SmartArt for diagrams/processes
- Add purposeful animations

**Making Slides Dynamic**:
- One key message per slide
- Use SmartArt to visualize content
- Replace sentences with images/animations
- Create visual emphasis points
- Use color dynamically and purposefully

### 5.2 Learning Activity Design

Activities transfer content from theory to practice. Seven main activity types:

**1. Group Discussion**
- Purpose: Share experiences, explore perspectives
- Best for: Topics where learners have existing knowledge
- Structure: Pose question → small group discussion → plenary sharing → facilitator synthesis

**2. Case Study**
- Purpose: Apply knowledge to realistic scenarios
- Best for: Problem-solving and decision-making skills
- Structure: Present case → individual/group analysis → present solutions → facilitator debrief

**3. Practice/Exercise**
- Purpose: Hands-on skill application
- Best for: Technical or procedural skills
- Structure: Demo → guided practice → independent practice → feedback

**4. Role Play**
- Purpose: Practice interpersonal skills in safe environment
- Best for: Communication, negotiation, customer service skills
- Structure: Set scenario → assign roles → perform → observe → debrief

**5. Crossword/Word Games**
- Purpose: Reinforce terminology and concepts
- Best for: Knowledge retention and review
- Structure: Present puzzle → individual/pair work → reveal answers → discuss

**6. Brainstorming**
- Purpose: Generate ideas, explore possibilities
- Best for: Creative problem-solving, opening topics
- Structure: State topic → free idea generation (no criticism) → organize/categorize → prioritize

**7. Mind Mapping**
- Purpose: Organize and connect concepts visually
- Best for: Complex topics with many interconnections
- Structure: Central concept → branch ideas → sub-branches → cross-connections → present

### Activity Design Criteria (REAL)
Every activity must be:
- **R**elevant: Directly relates to course content and learner context
- **E**ngaging: Captures interest and motivates participation
- **A**ctive: Learners are doing, creating, solving — not just watching
- **L**earner-centered: Builds on and leverages learner experience

## 6. Step 4: Implementation

Pilot the course with a small group before full rollout. Observe what works, what confuses learners, where timing is off. Collect feedback on content relevance, activity effectiveness, and overall experience.

---

## 7. Step 5: Evaluation and Revision

Review the course holistically after implementation. Use the 18-criterion evaluation checklist (see `evaluation-criteria.md`) to assess quality across all dimensions. Revise based on findings, then re-pilot if changes are significant.
