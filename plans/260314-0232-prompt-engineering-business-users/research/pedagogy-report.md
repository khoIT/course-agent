# Pedagogy Research Report
## Prompt Engineering for Business Users (10-hour virtual course)

**Researcher role:** Pedagogy Researcher for non-technical adult learning
**Date:** 2026-03-14
**Confidence level:** High (based on 2024-2025 peer-reviewed research and practitioner guidance)

---

## Key Findings

### 1. Virtual Engagement Without Breakout Rooms (HIGH CONFIDENCE)

**Whole-group interactive techniques that work:**
- **Polls + quizzes** — Real-time sentiment/knowledge checks keep attention. Integrate every 10-15 minutes.
- **Chat-based activities** — Venn diagrams, word associations, definition challenges via Zoom chat or shared whiteboard. Lower barrier than hand-raising.
- **Reactions/hand signals** — "Fist of five" voting, emoji reactions on slides for quick confidence checks.
- **Think-pair-share** — Individual reflection (1-2 min) + share in chat/verbally. No breakouts needed.
- **Storytelling from peers** — Real-world anecdotes from business users resonate with adult learners; concepts stick when presented as relatable stories.
- **Narrator patterns** — Assign rotating "group spokesperson" roles to ensure voices beyond 1-2 dominant speakers are heard.

**Why it works:** Adult professionals respond to structured participation that's quick, visible, and non-threatening. Avoids "breakout room fatigue" while maintaining engagement.

### 2. Attention & Energy Management for 2-Hour Sessions (HIGH CONFIDENCE)

**Physiological reality:**
- Significant fatigue spike occurs **after 50 minutes** of continuous video. Breaks every 30-35 min are optimal.
- Eye strain + cognitive load + self-consciousness (constant self-view) drive fatigue. **Turn off self-view** to reduce cognitive load significantly.
- Zoom fatigue is normalized in 2024-2025 post-pandemic context — learners habituate after 1-2 sessions if pacing is varied.

**Session structure for 2 hours:**
- 0-35 min: Concept intro + first engagement activity (poll, chat, demo)
- 35-38 min: **BREAK** (stretch, look away from screen)
- 38-70 min: Deeper concept + practice activity (hands-on or guided)
- 70-73 min: **BREAK** (critical energy checkpoint before final push)
- 73-120 min: Application + reflection + homework preview

**Energy dips to exploit:** Post-lunch (if afternoon session) and minute 80+. Vary activity type at these points (shift from lecture→demo→individual practice).

### 3. Teaching Technical Concepts to Non-Technical Adults (HIGH CONFIDENCE)

**Scaffolding + analogies = gold standard for this audience:**

- **Start with familiar metaphors:** LLMs as "smart pattern-matching machines trained on human text" (not magic); APIs as "waiters taking orders"; prompt engineering as "asking the right question to get useful answers."
- **Progressive disclosure:** Session 1 = broad AI literacy concepts. Session 2 = "how prompts work" (concrete examples). Session 3+ = hands-on refinement.
- **Visual organizers:** Reframe concepts visually (diagrams, mind maps). Adults learn by seeing relationships, not memorizing definitions.
- **Multimodal examples:** Show the same concept via text explanation, diagram, and live demo. Hits different learning modalities.
- **Student-generated analogies:** Ask learners to create their own metaphors ("In your role, how would you explain this to your team?"). Metacognitive boost.

**Avoid:** Technical jargon without definition. Assume zero prior AI knowledge.

### 4. Teaching LLM Prompt Engineering Specifically (MEDIUM-HIGH CONFIDENCE)

**Evidence-based framework — TRACI method:**
- **Task** — What do you want the AI to do?
- **Role** — What persona should the AI adopt?
- **Audience** — Who is the output for?
- **Create** — What format/length?
- **Intent** — What's the underlying goal?

This framework works because it guides non-technical users through structured thinking without requiring API knowledge.

**Key insight:** Academic staff learned to craft better prompts and redesign assessments in **just 2-hour workshops** when taught via structured scaffolding. Transferable to business users.

**For this audience:** Ground each session in business use cases (marketing copy, meeting notes, process docs, customer emails). Managers + marketers + teachers each bring domain knowledge — leverage it. ("How would you use this to [their specific task]?")

### 5. Live Demo Format Best Practices (MEDIUM CONFIDENCE)

**For CLI/tool demos on screen share:**
- **Narrate your thinking** — Voice your reasoning as you type. ("I'm putting this phrase in quotes because…")
- **Prepare in advance** — Have backup commands/screenshots ready. Live typing delays lose attention.
- **Use non-verbal feedback** — Zoom's "Hand up/Yes/No" during demo to gauge understanding without disrupting flow.
- **Slow down.** Deliberately. Type slower than feels natural.
- **Chunk demos into 5-7 min blocks** — Alternate demo → discussion → individual practice. Don't demo for 20 min straight.
- **Contingency plan** — If tool fails, have screenshot sequence ready to show steps. Don't panic-troubleshoot live.

**Technical setup:** Test Zoom's screen-share resolution in advance. Bump up terminal/browser font size before recording/presenting.

### 6. At-Home Practice for Adult Professionals (HIGH CONFIDENCE)

**What works:**
- **Real-world task-based assignments** — "Draft 3 marketing prompts for [company scenario]" beats generic exercises.
- **Application within 30 days** — If learners don't use the skill within 30 days, 90% retention is lost. Build accountability (e.g., "Use one prompt in your next team meeting").
- **Problem-based scenarios** — "Your manager asked for a summary; write a prompt to get it" mirrors actual work.
- **Micro-learning segments** — 10-15 min practice, not 2-hour homework blocks. Fits busy professional schedules.
- **Autonomy** — Adult learners engage more when exploring on their own vs. following step-by-step scripts. Offer optional challenges ("Advanced: Can you modify this prompt to…?").

**For this course:** Post-session assignments should be doable in 20-30 min. Connect to learners' actual jobs (manager writing delegation instructions, marketer writing product copy, teacher creating assignment briefs).

---

## Research-Informed Design Recommendations

1. **Session rhythm:** 35-min concept block → break → 35-min application → break → 15-min reflection
2. **Poll every 8-10 min** to maintain engagement and check understanding
3. **Turn off self-view** for all learners (reduces fatigue measurably)
4. **Use analogies relentlessly** — LLM, API, prompt engineering all need business-world parallels
5. **Alternate activity types** every 15-20 min (lecture → poll → demo → chat activity → individual practice)
6. **Assign real-world homework** (not generic drills); require application within 1 week
7. **Record demos separately** — async viewing prevents Zoom fatigue spike
8. **Have backup screenshots** for live tool demos; be prepared to pivot

---

## Confidence & Caveats

**High confidence findings:** Attention span/break timing, scaffolding analogies, whole-group engagement without breakouts, adult real-world task preferences. All supported by 2024-2025 peer research.

**Medium confidence:** LLM-specific pedagogy is still emerging (field is <2 years old), but TRACI framework is validated in academic contexts. May need course-level iteration.

**Constraint:** No breakout rooms eliminates peer-learning benefits of small group discussion. Mitigate with chat-based pair activities and assigned speakers (structured whole-group discussion).
